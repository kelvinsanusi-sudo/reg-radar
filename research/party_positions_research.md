## AI Regulation

- Labour government (2024 manifesto / King's Speech) signalled "binding regulation
  on the handful of companies developing the most powerful AI models". By Feb 2025,
  shifted to: "most AI systems should be regulated at the point of use" via existing
  sector regulators rather than a single AI law. On 28 April 2026, Tech Secretary
  Liz Kendall set out two priorities: supporting British AI companies and working
  internationally on standards for AI deployment. No standalone AI Bill has passed yet.
- Liberal Democrats: focus on "Strategic Sovereign Public Procurement" - ensuring
  critical national functions (datacentres, databases) run on UK-controlled tech,
  plus local "AI sandboxes" for startups/SMEs to test services.
- Greens: favour a precautionary, EU-aligned regulatory approach addressing bias,
  discrimination, equality, and privacy harms from AI.
- Conservatives and Reform UK: no specific AI regulation policy found; Reform's
  manifesto did not mention AI.

## Online Safety

- Labour: wants to build on the Online Safety Act 2023, with further measures on
  online crime, hate, extremism, fraud, gambling, deepfakes, and giving coroners
  access to tech firms' data after a child's death.
- Conservatives: also want to build on the Act, focused on restricting children's
  access to social media and putting school phone bans on a statutory footing.
- Reform UK: pledged (28 July 2025) to scrap the Online Safety Act entirely,
  calling it a threat to free speech, while separately proposing child-friendly
  restricted phones and under-16 school phone bans.
- Lib Dems / Greens: no specific Online Safety Act position found in this search.

## Data Protection & Privacy

- The Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 (DUAA) received Royal Assent 19 July 2025,
  reforming UK GDPR/DPA 2018, enabling a digital identity verification system and
  a National Data Library, intended to ease the more controversial elements of the
  prior Conservative government's DPDI Bill.
- Labour: introduced the DUA Bill (as the Data (Use and Access) Bill) following the
  2024 King's Speech; positions it as enabling economic growth and better public
  services while addressing prior criticisms of the Conservatives' approach.
- Conservatives: their earlier DPDI Bill (and DPDI No.2 Bill) failed to pass before
  the 2024 election; DUAA effectively supersedes this.
- Reform UK: no specific DUAA policy position found beyond standard privacy notices.

## Digital Markets & Competition

- The Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act 2024 (DMCCA) received Royal
  Assent 24 May 2024 and is being implemented in phases through 2025-2026 (CMA
  digital markets regime, conduct requirements for firms with "Strategic Market
  Status", alternative dispute resolution rules).
- No clear party-by-party positions were found for 2026 - this is largely treated
  as ongoing technical implementation of an Act passed under the prior government,
  continued by Labour, rather than a live partisan battleground.

## Social Media Regulation

- Labour: proposals on online crime, hate, extremism, fraud, gambling, and
  deepfakes; notably 107 Labour MPs abstained on a House of Lords amendment on
  social media regulation, showing internal division.
- Conservatives: focus on restricting children's social media access and statutory
  school phone bans.
- Reform UK: child-friendly restricted phones, under-16 school phone bans; broadly
  opposes mandating algorithm disclosure to regulators (consistent with
  free-market, light-touch approach).
- Lib Dems: propose a new "Online Crime Agency" to tackle illegal content (fraud,
  revenge porn, incitement to violence).
- Greens: no specific position found in this search.

## AI in Public Services

- Labour government published the "AI Opportunities Action Plan" (Jan 2025):
  20x expansion of sovereign compute by 2030, piloting AI across public services,
  "AI Growth Zones", and a National Data Library to unlock public sector data.
  Over £1bn committed to AI development via the National AI Strategy.
- A dedicated AI Bill has been repeatedly signalled but not introduced; no
  system-wide requirement to monitor where AI is used across the public sector,
  and no proactive regulator for public-sector AI use as of mid-2026. Legislation
  not expected before late 2026 at the earliest.
- No specific opposition party positions on AI-in-public-services found in this
  search.
